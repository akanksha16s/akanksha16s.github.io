Whispers from the Wild

On silent presence and true worship

There are some moments you don’t realise are important when they are happening. They just sit quietly somewhere in your memory, and later you notice, they changed something.

One such moment happened in Udaipur, Rajasthan. We were staying inside the Sajjangarh Wildlife Sanctuary for a conference. It was late at night, and I couldn’t sleep. That phase after COVID… I used to have some anxieties. So I stepped out, just to walk, thinking it might help.

The place was quiet in a very different way. Not empty—just still. After walking for a while, I saw a few guards standing on a slightly raised structure. It wasn’t exactly a terrace, but high enough to look over the area. It felt odd that they were just standing there, so I went closer and climbed up.

And then I saw it. A glorious TIGER.

It was just there. Standing. Not doing anything. I don’t remember feeling scared. That’s what I find strange even now. It was such a powerful animal, right there, and yet there was no panic in me. I was just… still.

"The way it stood, calm, almost effortless. Like everything around it already belonged to it, and it didn’t need to prove it. It felt like the knight of the jungle had come to visit his own territory."

It looked around slowly, and then at one point, it looked up, towards us. I don’t know how long that moment lasted. Probably a few seconds. But I remember my eyes filling up. I had already seen tigers before, in zoos. But this was not just any tiger. It was the knight of the jungle. Something about it felt too real. Too complete. Too majestic.

***

A few months later, something else happened. I was in the forests of the Palani Hills, near Madurai. We were just passing through, and what stood out immediately was that the space still belonged to the animals. Humans hadn’t fully taken over it yet. There was a kind of balance—quiet, undisturbed.

There were monkeys everywhere. Not aggressive like the ones we are habitual of, but just sitting, resting, watching. Then I saw a baby monkey. It was sitting in its mother’s lap. Very small, probably just a few months old. Just watching. I don’t know why, but I stopped.

It kept looking at me, and then it tapped its mother and pointed towards us. It kept looking back and forth, like it was trying to understand what we were—two-legged creatures with different coloured skins.

And in that moment, something felt very clear. The jungle had expressed itself to me. I was no longer the one observing—it was me who was being seen, understood, maybe even questioned. I felt… honoured. Like I was chosen to witness the jungle.

Being someone who doesn’t really believe in religion or conventional ideas of God, these moments made me question the kind of world we are living in. People constantly trying to prove the superiority of their beliefs, their religions, their versions of truth. But standing there, in those moments, none of that felt real. It all felt… unnecessary.

No one really knows what spirituality is. No one really knows what religion is. And yet, we fight over it. For me, these incidents made me believe in something far more quietly. Not through scriptures. Not through stories. But through experience.

I think if there is a god, it is probably like that. Not loud. Not demanding. Not dividing. Just there. Existing. And maybe, that is where the real power lies. In simple existence…

I have rarely felt the urge to go to a house of worship. I almost never feel like folding my hands in front of a deity. But every time I see mountains covered in snow, I feel like kneeling down.

Every morning, when the first ray of the sun touches me, I feel like bowing to it. Not because I want anything in return. But because in those moments, I feel humbled… grounded… almost pure.

And if that is not what true worship is, then I don’t know what is.
